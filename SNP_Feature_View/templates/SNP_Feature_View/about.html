About SNP See

SNP See was developed by David Muller as a project for BIMM 185 (Bioinformatics Lab) at UC San Diego. SNP See's major aim is to provide an entryway for anyone--scientist or not--to learn more about the human genome. To do this, we've chosen to highlight human traits that are determined by SNPs ("Single-Nucleotide Polymorphisms").

What does that mean? Well, at a high level, the human genom consists of 3 billion A's, T's, C's, and G's arranged in a specific order. In a strictly genetic sense (A's, T's, C's, and G's), humans are more than 99% similar from individual to individual. Interestingly, much of the 1% "difference" between humans is made up by SNPs--isolated, single letter differences between genomes. To clarify: at some given position of the genome, everyone usually has the same letter (A, T, C or G). In a SNP, some individuals might have one letter (say A), and other individuals may have another (say G).

These incredibly small differences (1 in 3 billion!), can sometimes have dramatic effects at a level we can all see. For instance, resistance to a certain type of malaria is determined by a single SNP. SNP See aims to use phenomenon determined by SNPs as an entry point for people to learn more about what the human genome is, and what it contains.
